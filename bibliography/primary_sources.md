# Primary Sources from "Epistemology of Logic: Logic-Dialectic or Theory of Knowledge"

This document compiles ALL primary philosophical sources explicitly referenced by Epameinondas Xenopoulos in his work "Epistemology of Logic" (2nd ed., 2024, Kefalonia). It combines and reconciles information from both the English and Greek versions of the source list.

## Ancient Greek Philosophy

**Parmenides of Elea** (Παρμενίδης ο Ελεάτης): Cited for the "Parmenidean" static principle of identity (Α = Α) and as the origin of formal-static logical thought (pp. 10, 15, 33, 48).

**Heraclitus of Ephesus** (Ηράκλειτος ο Εφέσιος): Cited for the "Heraclitean" dynamic-dialectical principle of contradiction and flux ("everything flows", τὰ πάντα ῥεῖ) (pp. 10, 15, 20, 48, 114-116, 274).

**Zeno of Elea** (Ζήνων ο Ελεάτης): Referenced in the context of Eleatic dialectics (pp. 105-114).

**Pyrrho** (Πύρρων): Referenced in the context of skepticism and its relation to dialectical thought (Greek version only).

**Socrates** (Σωκράτης): Mentioned for the method of inductive arguments and seeking universal definitions (p. 122).

**Plato** (Πλάτων): Discussed extensively for his dialectical method (διαλεκτική), theory of Ideas (εἶδος/ἰδέα), and his role in the historical development of logical thought (pp. 20, 118-119, 122).

**Aristotle** (Αριστοτέλης): Fundamental source. Cited as the founder of formal (traditional) logic ("Organon"), for the principles of identity, non-contradiction, the excluded middle, for syllogistic theory, taxonomy, and the concept of substance (οὐσία) (pp. 18, 20-21, 122-140, 271).

## Modern & Enlightenment Philosophy

**Francis Bacon** (Φραγκίσκος Βέργιος): Referenced for his "Novum Organum" and views on logic as the art of discovery (pp. 21, 39). *[Greek version only]*

**René Descartes** (Ρενέ Ντεκάρτ): Mentioned in the historical development of logic and methodology (p. 21).

**Baruch Spinoza** (Μπαρούχ Σπινόζα): Mentioned, including the dictum "omnis determinatio est negatio" (pp. 21, 54).

**Gottfried Wilhelm Leibniz** (Γκότφριντ Βίλχελμ Λάιμπνιτς): Discussed for his project of a universal formal language ("Characteristica Universalis") and "Ars Combinatoria," seen as a precursor to mathematical logic (pp. 21-22, 30-31, 147-151).

**Arthur Schopenhauer** (Άρτουρ Σοπενχάουερ): Mentioned in relation to Kantian philosophy and its development (Greek version only).

**Immanuel Kant** (Ιμμάνουελ Καντ): Cited for his views on logic, the categories, and his statement that logic had not advanced since Aristotle (pp. 21, 152-153).

## German Idealism & 19th Century Philosophy

**Georg Wilhelm Friedrich Hegel** (Γκέοργκ Βίλχελμ Φρίντριχ Χέγκελ): A primary source. Extensively discussed for his systematic dialectical logic, the concept of becoming (γίγνεσθαι), the universal (U), and the positive role of contradiction (pp. 10, 22, 24, 33-34, 39, 172-177, 210-212, 274-276).

**Karl Marx** (Καρλ Μαρξ): A foundational source. Cited for the materialist inversion of Hegelian dialectics, the concept of praxis, and the historical-genetic method. His mathematical manuscripts (MMM) are specifically noted (pp. 22, 26, 30-31, 39, 212-219, 276-277).

**Friedrich Engels** (Φρίντριχ Ένγκελς): Cited alongside Marx for developing materialist dialectics and for his works "Anti-Dühring" and "Dialectics of Nature" (pp. 22, 27-28, 31, 38).

**George Boole** (Τζορτζ Μπουλ): Referenced as a founder of mathematical (symbolic) logic and for Boolean algebra (pp. 24, 153-155).

**Gottlob Frege** (Γκότλομπ Φρέγκε): Cited for his contributions to the foundations of mathematical logic and formal language (pp. 24, 30, 41, 155-160).

## 20th Century Philosophy & Logic

**Bertrand Russell** (Μπέρτραντ Ράσελ): Mentioned for his work (with Whitehead) in "Principia Mathematica" and logical atomism (pp. 24, 167-168).

**David Hilbert** (Ντάβιντ Χίλμπερτ): Cited for his formalist program and axiomatic method in the foundations of mathematics (pp. 24, 160-166).

**Jan Łukasiewicz** (Γιαν Λουκασέβιτς): Referenced for his development of multi-valued and three-valued logics (pp. 18, 25, 35).

**Jean Piaget** (Ζαν Πιαζέ): Extensively discussed and cited for his genetic epistemology and the operational structures of thought (INRC group), which Xenopoulos uses and extends (pp. 12-13, 23, 25-26, 44, 48).

**Vladimir Lenin** (Βλαντίμιρ Λένιν): Cited for his philosophical contributions to materialist dialectics and epistemology, particularly in his "Philosophical Notebooks" (pp. 22-23, 29-30, 33-34, 39, 43, 45).

**Roman Suszko** (Ρόμαν Σούσκο): Referenced for his work on non-Fregean logic and formal approaches to dialectical contradiction (pp. 18, 25, 35, 45).

**L. Rogowski** (Λ. Ρογκόβσκι): Cited for his work on directional logic and formalizing Hegelian theses on contradiction (pp. 18, 25, 45). *[Greek version only]*

**D. Dubarle** (Ντ. Ντυμπάρλ): Mentioned for his interpretations and formalizations related to dialectical logic (pp. 25, 35, 49). *[Greek version only]*

**Alfred Tarski** (Άλφρεντ Τάρσκι): Mentioned in the context of semantic theory and the concept of truth (pp. 23, 28).

**Willard Van Orman Quine** (Γουίλαρντ βαν Όρμαν Κουάιν): Referenced in discussions on truth values and ontology (pp. 13, 24-25, 35).

**Jürgen Habermas** (Γιούργκεν Χάμπερμας): Referenced for his theory of communicative action (Greek version only).

## Modern Greek Thought

**Theodoros Antoniou** (Θεόδωρος Αντωνίου): Cited for his contributions to the development of dialectical logic in the Greek philosophical tradition (Greek version only).

## Key Greek Terms and Concepts from Xenopoulos's Text

This section lists crucial Greek philosophical terms used by Xenopoulos as primary conceptual sources, drawn directly from his vocabulary.

**Λόγος (Lógos):** Reason, discourse, the organizing principle of thought and reality (pp. 14-16).

**Γίγνεσθαι (Gígnesthai):** Becoming, the process of coming-to-be, central to dialectical thought (pp. 10, 15, 28, 34, 53).

**Εἶναι (Eînai):** Being, static existence (contrasted with γίγνεσθαι) (pp. 10, 15, 27).

**Διαλεκτική (Dialektikḗ):** Dialectics, the method and process of thought/reality through contradiction and synthesis.

**Ἀντίφασις (Antíphasis):** Contradiction, the central dynamic principle of dialectical logic (pp. 10, 26-27, 29, 37, 54).

**Οὐσία (Ousía):** Substance, essence (Aristotelian term) (p. 47).

**Πρότσες (Prótses):** Process (Xenopoulos's transliteration, used throughout) (pp. 10, 27, 34).

**Ὄν (On):** Being, entity (pp. 15, 48).

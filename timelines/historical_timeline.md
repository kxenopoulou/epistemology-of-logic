# Historical Timeline of Logical Development  
_from_ **Epistemology of Logic: Logic–Dialectic or Theory of Knowledge**

This timeline presents the historical development of logical thought as outlined by **Epameinondas Xenopoulos**, compiled exclusively from the chronological references of the book.

---

## Pre‑Aristotelian Foundations (6th–4th Century BCE)

### Pre‑Socratic Period

- **Heraclitus of Ephesus**  
  Dynamic‑dialectical principle *“everything flows”* (τὰ πάντα ῥεῖ);  
  foundation of dialectical thought with contradiction as a central principle  
  (pp. 10, 15, 20, 48, 114–116, 274)

- **Parmenides of Elea**  
  Static principle of identity *(A = A)*;  
  origin of formal‑static logical thought  
  (pp. 10, 15, 33, 48)

- **Zeno of Elea**  
  Eleatic dialectics and paradoxes  
  (pp. 105–114)

- **Eleatic School**  
  Development of the dialectical method  
  (pp. 105–114)

---

## Classical Greek Philosophy

- **Socrates**  
  Method of inductive reasoning and the search for universal definitions  
  (p. 122)

- **Plato**  
  Dialectical method (*διαλεκτική*), theory of Ideas (*εἶδος / ἰδέα*),  
  and decisive role in the historical development of logical thought  
  (pp. 20, 118–119, 122)

- **Sophists**  
  Development of dialectical techniques  
  (pp. 116–117)

---

## Aristotelian Foundation (4th Century BCE)

- **Aristotle** (384–322 BCE)  
  Foundation of formal (traditional) logic through the *Organon*;  
  development of syllogistic theory and the principles of identity,  
  non‑contradiction, and excluded middle;  
  taxonomic organization of thought  
  (pp. 20–21, 122–140, 271)

- Considered by **Immanuel Kant** as the point beyond which logic had not advanced  
  (p. 21)

---

## Post‑Aristotelian Development (3rd Century BCE – 17th Century CE)

### Hellenistic Period

- **Stoic School**  
  Development of propositional logic  
  (pp. 140–142)

### Medieval Period

- **Scholastic Logic**  
  Continuation and systematic development of Aristotelian logic  
  through the 17th century  
  (pp. 142–147)

### Early Modern Period

- **Francis Bacon** (1561–1626)  
  *Novum Organum* and logic as an art of discovery  
  (pp. 21, 39)

- **René Descartes** (1596–1650)  
  Contributions to method and logical development  
  (p. 21)

- **Baruch Spinoza** (1632–1677)  
  Formulation of the dictum *omnis determinatio est negatio*  
  (pp. 21, 54)

---

## Enlightenment & German Idealism (17th–19th Centuries)

- **Gottfried Wilhelm Leibniz** (1646–1716)  
  Project of a universal formal language (*Characteristica Universalis*)  
  and *Ars Combinatoria*;  
  precursor of mathematical logic as logical calculus  
  (pp. 21–22, 30–31, 147–151)

- **Immanuel Kant** (1724–1804)  
  Doctrine of categories and the thesis that logic had not advanced  
  beyond Aristotle  
  (pp. 21, 152–153)

- **Georg Wilhelm Friedrich Hegel** (1770–1831)  
  Systematic development of idealist dialectical logic;  
  concepts of becoming, the universal (U), and the positive role of contradiction  
  (pp. 10, 22, 24, 33–34, 39, 172–177, 210–212, 274–276)

---

## 19th‑Century Materialist Dialectics

- **Karl Marx** (1818–1883)  
  Materialist inversion of Hegelian dialectics;  
  historical‑genetic method and mathematical manuscripts (MMM)  
  (pp. 22, 26, 30–31, 39, 212–219, 276–277)

- **Friedrich Engels** (1820–1895)  
  Co‑development of dialectical materialism;  
  *Anti‑Dühring* and *Dialectics of Nature*  
  (pp. 22, 27–28, 31, 38)

---

## Development of Mathematical Logic (Mid‑19th – Early 20th Century)

### Foundational Period

- **George Boole** (1815–1864)  
  Founder of mathematical (symbolic) logic; Boolean algebra  
  (pp. 24, 153–155)

- **Augustus De Morgan** (1806–1871)  
  Contributions to mathematical logic  
  (p. 24)

### Late 19th – Early 20th Century

- **Gottlob Frege** (1848–1925)  
  Foundations of mathematical logic and formal language  
  (pp. 24, 30, 41, 155–160)

- **Giuseppe Peano** (1858–1932)  
  Foundations of mathematics and symbolic logic  
  (pp. 24, 166–167)

- **David Hilbert** (1862–1943)  
  Formalist program and axiomatic method  
  (pp. 24, 160–166)

---

## Early 20th‑Century Logic

- **Bertrand Russell** (1872–1970)  
  *Principia Mathematica* (with Whitehead) and logical atomism  
  (pp. 24, 167–168)

- **Alfred North Whitehead** (1861–1947)  
  Co‑author of *Principia Mathematica*  
  (p. 24)

- **Clarence Irving Lewis** (1883–1964)  
  Modal logic  
  (pp. 18, 35)

- **Jan Łukasiewicz** (1878–1956)  
  Many‑valued and three‑valued logics  
  (pp. 18, 25, 35)

---

## 20th‑Century Developments & Dialectical Logic

- **Vladimir Lenin** (1870–1924)  
  Contributions to materialist dialectics;  
  insights from *Philosophical Notebooks*  
  (pp. 22–23, 29–30, 33–34, 39, 43, 45)

### Logical Schools & Movements

- **Vienna Circle**  
  Logical positivism  
  (p. 19)

- **Warsaw School**  
  Development of modern logic  
  (p. 19)

### Mid‑20th‑Century Logic

- **Arend Heyting** (1898–1980) — Intuitionistic logic (p. 35)  
- **Kurt Gödel** (1906–1978) — Incompleteness theorems (pp. 28, 35)  
- **Alonzo Church** (1903–1995) — Syntactic limits of formal systems (p. 28)  
- **Alfred Tarski** (1901–1983) — Semantic theory of truth (pp. 23, 28)  
- **Stephen C. Kleene** (1909–1994) — Metamathematics (p. 35)  
- **Evert Willem Beth** (1908–1964) — Foundations of mathematics (p. 35)  
- **Haskell B. Curry** (1900–1982) — Combinatory logic (p. 35)  
- **Robert Feys** (1889–1961) — Combinatory logic (p. 35)  
- **Gerhard Gentzen** (1909–1945) — Proof theory (p. 35)  
- **Frederic B. Fitch** (1908–1987) — Modal logic (p. 35)

## Genetic Epistemology & Contemporary Synthesis

- **Jean Piaget** (1896–1980)

  (pp. 18, 25, 35, 45)

- **Willard Van Orman Quine** (1908–2000)

  (pp. 13, 24–25, 35)

## Epameinondas Xenopoulos (1923–1994)

- **Epameinondas Xenopoulos**  
  Author of *Epistemology of Logic: Logic–Dialectic or Theory of Knowledge*;  
  synthesis of 2,500 years of Greek philosophical thought into modern  
  mathematical frameworks;  
  development of formal‑dialectical logic integrating static and dynamic dimensions;  
  work completed posthumously by family  
  (1st ed. 1998, 2nd ed. 2024)
